Drawing Congressional Districts from 1-Square-Mile Squares

Where the idea stands after testing it on four very different states — and the one decision left to make.

The idea, in one paragraph

Lay a grid of one-square-mile blocks (640 acres each) over a state. Starting in the middle, add blocks outward — up, down, left, right — until that district holds its fair share of the population. Then start the next district, and the next, using the exact same process for every state. The goal: as many clean, square-ish districts as possible, with only a couple of odd-shaped leftovers.

It works. Using real 2020 U.S. Census data (down to the city-block level), the engine builds districts that are within a fraction of a percent of perfectly equal population, all fully connected, by an identical, repeatable, tamper-proof process. On Colorado, the biggest district is off from a perfectly equal share by about 172 people out of 721,714.

Your idea, across four very different states

We deliberately picked four hard-in-different-ways states. Each picture is the real computed result — every colored region is one congressional district built from square-mile blocks, starting from the middle and growing out.

Notice Florida: one district (the leftover) gets smeared across the whole state. That single picture is the open problem — explained below.

The headline. Your center-out, squares-first idea (top row) is the best squares-based method and passes Colorado, Maryland, and New York cleanly. It fails only Florida. The plain "shortest-line" textbook method passes everywhere — but it produces the most odd-shaped districts and contains none of the center-out, square-block idea. So the real choice is between finishing your idea and settling for the textbook baseline.

Why Florida — and only Florida — breaks

It's a single, well-understood issue we call the leftover problem. As districts grow outward and claim their share, the last district has to absorb whatever land is still unclaimed. In Florida that leftover isn't in one place — it's the panhandle and the Keys and bits of coast all at once, so the final district ends up as a thin shape stretched across the state (the gray-ish smear in the Florida picture). Colorado, Maryland, and New York don't have that scattered-leftover geography, which is why they pass. This is a fixable mechanics problem, not a flaw in the idea.

What makes your idea worth finishing

Compare the same state two ways. Left is your center-out square method; right is the textbook baseline that currently passes everywhere. Both are perfectly legal and equal-population — but one looks like the idea you described, and one doesn't.

Recommendation

Finish the idea with a "hybrid ending." Keep your center-out square growth for all but the last couple of districts, then draw those final leftover seats with the textbook shortest-line method — which happens to be excellent at exactly the scattered-leftover case your method struggles with. It's a small, surgical change that targets the only failure (Florida) without touching the three states that already pass. The likely result: your idea becomes the winner everywhere, as both the squares-first and the center-out choice.

The alternatives, honestly:

  • Ship the textbook baseline now. It scales to all 50 states today — but it throws away the entire center-out, square-block concept.
  • Best method per state. Rejected: it breaks the core promise that the same process runs on every state.

For us to discuss

  1. Do we invest in the "hybrid ending" to make the center-out idea win everywhere, or is the textbook baseline good enough to move forward now?
  2. How square is square enough? We currently flag a district as "odd-shaped" on a fixed rule — is that the right bar for what we're trying to show?
  3. For the big-city states (New York, California, Illinois, New Jersey), are we comfortable with the "flagged" treatment, or do we want the finer half-mile refinement for dense areas?
  4. What's the goal of the finished piece — a proof-of-concept on a handful of states, or the full 50-state run?
