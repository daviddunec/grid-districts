No softballs

Hard questions, straight answers

The strongest objections we know of — including the ones that have real teeth — answered the same way the report answers them for policymakers, minus the footnotes.

Doesn’t this hurt minority representation under the Voting Rights Act?

It would if these maps replaced the real ones — that’s exactly why the proposal doesn’t do that. A demographics-blind map can’t guarantee the minority-opportunity districts the VRA sometimes requires, so states keep drawing their own maps under existing law. The baseline is published next to the enacted map, and VRA-driven choices simply become documented, justified departures. The draft bill contains an explicit savings clause: the baseline binds no one and preempts nothing.

What about communities — city limits, county lines, neighborhoods?

The rule ignores them, on purpose. Every “respect this boundary” input is a knob, and history shows every knob gets turned by whoever holds the pen. The baseline trades community-boundary fit for something no hand-drawn map can offer: proof that nobody’s thumb was on the scale. States that value county integrity can keep it in their enacted maps — and explain the trade-off in public, with numbers.

Why squares? People don’t live in squares.

Squares are equal-area, identical everywhere, and impossible to nudge for advantage. One square mile is small enough to follow real population patterns (cities get many crowded squares, ranchland gets empty ones) but large enough that the math stays simple and checkable. The squares are just the unit we count in — like pixels in a photo.

Isn’t the deviation worse than real enacted maps?

Yes — and that’s disclosed everywhere it appears. (Deviation means how far a district’s population strays from a perfectly equal share.) Enacted maps reach near-zero deviation by splitting individual city blocks, a refinement step this build intentionally leaves to the official process. The benchmark’s job is to show the shape of politics-free lines; the report specifies exactly how a custodian would do the final block-level trim.

Why is New York flagged?

Because we don’t hide bad numbers. New York’s densest square mile holds about 121,000 people — roughly a sixth of a whole district’s 776,971-person share — and the rule never splits a square. Cuts that land near such heavy blocks can’t fine-tune the count, so New York’s worst district deviates about 4.08% from equal; our self-imposed flag-it threshold is 2%. The fix — subdividing only the densest cells — is specified in the report. The other 49 states pass the deviation gate clean.

Couldn’t someone game the algorithm?

After publication? No: there are no parameters to tune, no randomness to fish, no inputs except published census counts. The code is open source; the output is deterministic; anyone can re-run it and compare hashes. The only way to change the map is to change where people actually live — which is the one thing a map is supposed to reflect. The sharper version of this question — couldn’t the rule itself have been picked to favor someone? — gets the next answer.

Couldn’t YOU have picked a rule that favors one side?

That’s the strongest objection, so here’s the straight answer. Every design choice (one-square-mile squares, split-in-halves, the tie-breaking order) is published with its reasoning, was made on neutral grounds — the algorithm has no party data to look at — and the development history, including every mistake, ships in the public defect log. The 1950–2020 demonstration also shows the same rule producing sane maps across eight different political eras; it wasn’t tuned to 2020’s politics. And because the proposal is a disclosure standard, not a mandate, anyone who suspects the rule can publish a rival fixed rule and let the two baselines be compared in the open. The point was never that this rule is sacred — it’s that the reference point should be some fixed, published rule instead of a person with a stake in the outcome.

Does this help Democrats or Republicans?

The algorithm can’t know — it never sees party data. Geography does correlate with partisanship (cities lean one way, rural areas the other), so a blind map still has partisan consequences, and they differ by state. The honest answer is that a published baseline makes that question measurable instead of arguable: compare any enacted map to the baseline and the difference is the part politicians chose. Both parties gerrymander; this measures both equally.

What about Alaska, Hawaii, islands, and water?

Six states have a single at-large seat, so there’s nothing to draw. For island geography (Hawaii, Michigan’s peninsulas, the Florida Keys), the engine adds “virtual bridges” along real-world ferry and causeway connections so every district stays reachable. Hawaii’s two districts came out within 0.02% of an equal share.

Why isn’t Washington, D.C. (or Puerto Rico) on the map?

Because they have no voting House seats to draw — the 435 districts belong entirely to the 50 states. That’s also why our total is 330,759,736 rather than the famous full-U.S. census figure of 331,449,281: the difference is D.C.’s 689,545 residents, who have no House district under current law. (Puerto Rico’s 3.3 million residents are likewise outside the 435.) Whether they should have voting seats is a real debate — but it’s a different law than the one this project proposes.

Is this just the old “shortest splitline” idea?

The cutting rule builds on the splitline concept mathematicians proposed in the 2000s — we say so plainly. What’s new is everything that makes it usable: an exact-to-the-person accounting gate, island repair, a published verification suite, a 50-state run with cryptographic reproducibility, the 1950–2020 historical demonstration, and a draft disclosure law that fits current Supreme Court doctrine. An idea became an auditable system.

Has this actually been run, or is it a concept?

It’s run. All 50 states, 435 districts, zero failed runs, with the national total matching the official 50-state census total exactly: 330,759,736 people. Then it was re-run for every state in every census year back to 1950 — 348 runs in all. Every map on this site is real engine output, and the repository reproduces all of it.

Are these maps current, or stuck in 2020?

Both, on purpose. By law, districts are drawn from the once-a-decade census count — that’s the data with block-by-block precision, and it’s what every state’s real map uses too. But this site also carries the Census Bureau’s latest figures (July 1, 2025): every state page shows its 2025 population, the decade slider runs forward to a 2025-estimate map drawn by the same engine from the Bureau’s county estimates, and the home page previews which states would gain or lose seats on today’s numbers. When the 2030 census block data publishes, the whole site re-draws automatically.

Who controls the code?

Nobody, which is the point. It’s open source under the MIT license. The draft bill assigns a custodian (such as the Census Bureau) to run the published specification each cycle — but anyone on earth can run it too, which is what keeps the custodian honest.

How would this actually become law?

The draft bill (Appendix A of the report) is deliberately small: after each census, publish the baseline map for every state; require each enacted plan to be published alongside its baseline with a standard metrics comparison; require material deviations to be justified in writing. No district is forced on anyone. The report argues this fits squarely within Congress’s constitutional power to regulate House elections (the Elections Clause).

Can I check any of this myself?

Yes — that’s the entire design. The code, data pipeline, verification suite, defect log, and this website ship together as one open repository. Re-run any state and compare hashes (digital fingerprints of the output); re-derive any number in the report; the build fails loudly if a single person goes missing. Start with Run it yourself.
