The problem, in one minute

Whoever draws the lines picks the winners

Every ten years, each state redraws its congressional districts. In most states, the people drawing the lines have a direct stake in the result. Here’s why that matters — same fifty voters, three different maps:

An illustration, not real data: 30 Purple voters, 20 Gold voters, five equal districts of ten. All three maps are perfectly “legal.” Only the line-drawer changed.

This is called gerrymandering, and both parties do it wherever they hold the pen. The Supreme Court has said federal courts can’t police it (Rucho v. Common Cause, 2019). Every reform fight ends up stuck on the same question: who can be trusted to draw the lines?

The fix

Don’t trust anyone. Remove the mapmaker.

This project’s answer: a fixed, public recipe that turns census counts into districts — the same recipe for every state, with no knobs anyone can turn. Four steps:

1

Lay down a grid

Cover the state with squares of exactly one square mile (640 acres) — the same grid system every time.

2

Count the people

Fill each square with its official Census count. That number is the only thing the algorithm ever sees.

3

Split in halves

A fixed rule cuts the state in half by population, then halves the halves, until every district holds an equal share.

4

Prove it

Independent checks confirm every person landed in exactly one district — and that re-running it reproduces the map bit-for-bit.

The algorithm never sees race, party, income, or addresses. It can’t favor anyone, because it doesn’t know who anyone is. It only knows how many people live in each square mile.

It isn’t about today’s politics

Same rule, any era: 1950 → 2025

We ran the identical rule for every state in every census year since 1950 — 348 runs in all, zero failures — and then ran it forward onto the Census Bureau’s July 2025 estimates. Here’s Ohio shrinking from 23 seats to 15 as its share of the nation’s population fell. The process never changes; only the people move.

Maps for non-census years are estimates: today’s street-level population pattern, scaled to each year’s official county figures (details in About). The 2020 maps use full census-block data.

Updated with the latest numbers

America has kept moving since 2020

Districts are drawn from the once-a-decade census, but the Census Bureau estimates every state’s population each July. As of July 1, 2025, the 50 states hold an estimated 341,091,212 people — up 3.1% since the census. If the 435 seats were re-divided on those numbers today, here’s who would gain and lose:

📈 Gaining seats

Florida +2 (→ 30) · Texas +2 (→ 40) · Arizona +1 (→ 10) · Idaho +1 (→ 3) · Utah +1 (→ 5)

Fastest growth since 2020: Idaho +10.4%, Florida +8.9%, South Carolina +8.8%.

📉 Losing seats

Illinois -1 (→ 16) · Minnesota -1 (→ 7) · New York -1 (→ 25) · Oregon -1 (→ 5) · Rhode Island -1 (→ 1) · California -2 (→ 50)

Slowest growth since 2020: Hawaii -1.5%, West Virginia -1.5%, New York -1.0%.

And the maps keep up too: every state page’s decade slider now runs through 2025 — the same engine, drawn on the Bureau’s July 2025 county estimates (labeled as an estimate, like every non-census year).

The seat preview is a trend, not a prediction: official 2030 reapportionment will use the actual 2030 count. Seat math uses the official Huntington–Hill method — our implementation was verified by reproducing the real 2020 apportionment of all 435 seats exactly before touching the estimates. Source: U.S. Census Bureau, Vintage 2025 Population Estimates. The moment 2030 census data publishes, every map on this site rebuilds automatically.

What this is — and what it isn’t

A benchmark, not a ballot law

By law, real enacted maps must be almost perfectly equal in population. That last fraction of a percent takes a final fine-tuning step we deliberately leave to officials. So the proposal isn’t “enact these maps” — it’s “publish this neutral baseline, and make every state explain, in public, how far its map strays from it and why.”

The Voting Rights Act still applies

A demographics-blind map can’t, by itself, guarantee the districts the law sometimes requires so that minority voters can elect candidates of their choice. States keep drawing their own maps under existing law — the baseline just makes every departure visible and measurable. The draft bill says so explicitly, in a savings clause: a line stating that existing voting-rights law always wins.

One state is flagged, on purpose

New York’s densest square mile holds about 121,000 people — roughly a sixth of a whole district’s 776,971-person share — and the rule never splits a square. Cuts that land near such heavy blocks can’t fine-tune the count, so its worst district lands 4.08% from a perfectly equal share — above our self-imposed 2% flag threshold. 49 of 50 states pass the deviation gate clean. We publish the flag instead of hiding it; the fix is specified in the report.

We publish our mistakes

The project keeps a public defect log — 12 issues so far, each with its root cause and fix — plus an adversarial review trail. You shouldn’t have to trust us: every claim on this site can be re-derived from the open code and public census data.
