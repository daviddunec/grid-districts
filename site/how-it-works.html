The full walkthrough

How one rule draws every district in America

No committees, no consultants, no maps drawn behind closed doors. Just census counts, squares, and a splitting rule anyone can check. Here’s the whole thing, in plain English.

Step 1 — The only input: where people live

Every ten years, the Census Bureau counts everyone in America and publishes how many people live in each census block (a block is the smallest piece of census geography — often literally a city block). That public dataset is the algorithm’s entire input. Not race. Not party registration. Not election results. Not addresses. Just: how many people are here?

Step 2 — A grid of one-square-mile squares

The state is covered with a fixed grid of squares, each exactly one square mile (640 acres), laid out the same way every time on an equal-area map projection — so a square in Maine covers the same ground as a square in Arizona. Each census block’s population is placed into the square containing the block’s official center point. For Colorado, that’s 5,773,714 people distributed across the grid — and the grid total must match the state’s official census population exactly, or the build refuses to continue.

Step 3 — Cut in halves until done

To draw n districts, the rule splits the state into two regions — one side gets half the districts, the other side the rest — using the straight cut that balances population best. Then it does the same to each half, and keeps going until every region is exactly one district. The cut can run north–south, east–west, or diagonally; the rule always picks the one that balances people best, with every tie broken by a fixed, published order. There is no randomness anywhere — that’s why two strangers on two computers get byte-identical maps.

Step 4 — Clean up and prove it

A straight cut can occasionally strand a few cells — say, across a bay — from the rest of their district. A cleanup pass reattaches them to the nearest neighbor district, nudging populations back into balance along the way. Then the verification gauntlet runs:

  • Exact head-count: the sum of every district’s population must equal the state’s official census population to the last person — not approximately, exactly. All 50 states pass this exact head-count match.
  • No double-counting: every square mile belongs to exactly one district.
  • Connected districts: you can walk (or ferry) from any part of a district to any other.
  • Reproducibility: the engine is re-run — including with the input deliberately shuffled — and must produce a byte-identical map, verified with cryptographic hashes (a hash is a short digital fingerprint: change even one byte of the map and the fingerprint changes).

Nationally, the 435 districts account for 330,759,736 people — matching the official 50-state census total exactly.

What the rule deliberately ignores

City limits, county lines, freeways, party registration, where incumbents live, even good-faith communities of interest — the rule sees none of it. That blindness is the point: every one of those inputs is a knob, and every knob is an invitation to put a thumb on the scale. The cost is real (districts won’t hug county lines; see the FAQ), but the benefit is a reference map with nothing left to rig once the rule is published. (Could the rule itself have been chosen to favor someone? That’s a fair question — we answer it head-on in the FAQ.)

Known limits, stated plainly

  • It’s a benchmark. Enacted maps must reach near-zero population deviation; that final block-level refinement is specified but intentionally left to the official process. The proposal is a public yardstick, not a replacement mapmaker.
  • Dense cities strain one-mile squares. New York’s densest square mile holds about 121,000 people — roughly a sixth of a whole district’s 776,971-person share — and the rule never splits a square. Cuts that land near such heavy blocks can’t fine-tune the count, so New York’s worst district deviates 4.08%. 49 of 50 states come in under the 2% flag threshold; NY is flagged openly, and the refinement that fixes it is in the report.
  • The Voting Rights Act comes first. Where the law requires minority-opportunity districts, states keep drawing them; the baseline just measures and publicizes each departure. The draft bill has an explicit VRA savings clause.

Glossary

Census block

The smallest unit of census geography — often a literal city block in town, larger in the countryside. The 2020 census counted the population of every one of them.

Deviation

How far a district’s population is from a perfectly equal share. If a state with 8 seats has 8,000,000 people, the equal share is exactly 1,000,000; a district of 1,005,000 deviates +0.5%.

Deterministic

No randomness, no judgment calls: the same input always produces the exact same output. Anyone can re-run the code on public data and verify the maps byte-for-byte.

At-large state

A state with only one House seat (six of them in 2020), so the whole state is its district and there’s nothing to draw.

Benchmark / baseline

A neutral reference map published next to each state’s real, legally-enacted map — so everyone can see and measure exactly how far the real lines stray from politics-free ones.

Hash (digital fingerprint)

A short string of letters and numbers computed from a file. Change even one byte of the file and the hash changes completely — so two people comparing hashes can prove they have the exact same map without sending the whole thing.
